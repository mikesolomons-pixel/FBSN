Navigating Maneuvering through the unknown

Learning at speed and in motion, building the adaptive capacity to thrive in uncertainty.

Moving Between Where We Are and Where We Want to Be

Navigating is the play where strategy meets reality. It is how teams move between present conditions and future ambitions when the path is unclear, the terrain is shifting, and the map is incomplete. Most teams fail here — either paralyzed by the desire for certainty that never arrives, or charging ahead recklessly before they understand the landscape.

Effective navigation requires rhythm rather than rigid cadence. The pace of work should be driven by the nature of the challenge, not the calendar. Some problems demand rapid experimentation over days; others require patient observation over months. Teams that default to fixed quarterly planning cycles regardless of context are navigating by calendar, not by conditions — and the difference matters enormously.

A healthy team accumulates a growing scrap heap of failed experiments. This is not waste; it is evidence of genuine learning. Teams that never fail are not navigating — they are following well-worn paths that no longer lead anywhere interesting. The practice of set-based design helps here: holding multiple paths open simultaneously, testing hypotheses in parallel, and resisting the organizational pressure to commit to a single approach before the evidence supports it.

Core Principles

Rhythm over cadence — let the nature of the challenge dictate the pace, not the calendar
A growing scrap heap of failed experiments signals real learning, not dysfunction
Hold multiple paths open through set-based design rather than committing too early
Match the decision approach to the nature of the problem — not all challenges are alike
Build adaptive capacity: the ability to navigate uncertainty repeatedly, not just once
Resist the twin traps of analysis paralysis and reckless commitment

Practice 1

Cynefin Navigator

Automated

A decision framework based on Dave Snowden's Cynefin model that helps teams classify challenges by their nature and choose the right approach for each — because not all problems respond to the same kind of thinking.

Tutorial

▼

The Cynefin framework, developed by Dave Snowden, provides a sense-making model that helps teams understand the nature of the challenges they face and choose appropriate responses. The name comes from the Welsh word for "habitat" or "place of belonging" — reflecting the idea that context determines what kind of action is appropriate.

The framework identifies five domains. Clear (formerly "Obvious") is the domain of best practice, where cause and effect are self-evident. The response pattern is sense-categorize-respond: understand the situation, apply the known rule, execute. Most operational processes live here. Complicated is the domain of good practice, where cause and effect can be determined but require expertise. The pattern is sense-analyze-respond: bring in experts, evaluate options, choose the best approach. Engineering problems and technical challenges typically fall here.

Complex is the domain of emergent practice, where cause and effect can only be understood in retrospect. This is where most strategic challenges live. The response pattern is probe-sense-respond: run safe-to-fail experiments, observe what happens, amplify what works and dampen what does not. You cannot analyze your way to a solution in complexity — you must act to learn. Chaotic is the domain of novel practice, where cause and effect are unclear and the situation is urgent. The pattern is act-sense-respond: stabilize the situation first, then make sense of what happened. Crises and unprecedented disruptions land here.

The fifth domain, Confused (formerly "Disorder"), is the most dangerous. It is the state of not knowing which domain you are in — and therefore not knowing what kind of response is appropriate. The most common leadership failure is treating complex problems as if they were merely complicated: bringing in more analysis when what is needed is experimentation. The Cynefin Navigator helps teams avoid this trap by explicitly classifying each challenge before deciding how to address it.

In practice, teams use the navigator to map their portfolio of strategic challenges across the framework. This reveals patterns: an organization that treats everything as complicated is probably missing the emergent opportunities that only experimentation can uncover. A team that lives entirely in the complex domain may need to invest in standardizing what has already been learned. The framework does not prescribe answers — it prescribes the right kind of thinking for each situation.

Practice 2

Safe-to-Fail Experiments

Coming Soon

Designing structured probes for complex challenges — small, bounded experiments that generate real learning while limiting downside risk. Partially available through the Cynefin Navigator.

Tutorial

▼

When a challenge sits in the complex domain of Cynefin, traditional planning fails because the relationship between cause and effect cannot be determined in advance. Safe-to-fail experiments are the response: carefully designed probes that generate genuine learning while keeping the downside bounded and manageable.

Each experiment has a clear structure. It begins with a hypothesis — a testable statement about what the team believes might be true. Then a probe action: the specific, concrete thing the team will do to test that hypothesis. The probe should be small enough to execute quickly but substantial enough to generate meaningful data.

Critical to the design are the sensing mechanisms — how will the team know what happened? What will they measure, observe, or listen for? Without explicit sensing, experiments generate activity but not learning. The team also defines amplify signals (criteria for scaling what works) and dampen signals (criteria for stopping what does not). This prevents the common failure of continuing failed experiments out of sunk-cost bias or killing successful ones prematurely.

Finally, every experiment has safety bounds — explicit limits on time, resources, and exposure that define the maximum downside. A safe-to-fail experiment should be consequential enough to matter but bounded enough that failure is informative rather than catastrophic. The key insight is that these experiments run as a portfolio, not individual bets. The team expects most probes to fail; what matters is the pattern of learning that emerges across the portfolio.

Practice 3

Decision Architecture

Coming Soon

Structuring how the team makes high-stakes decisions — defining decision rights, matching speed to stakes, and building decision logs that accelerate organizational learning.

Tutorial

▼

Most leadership teams make decisions poorly not because the individuals lack judgment, but because the architecture of decision-making is poorly designed. Decision Architecture is the practice of deliberately structuring how a team approaches consequential choices — who decides, how fast, with what information, and how the outcome is captured for future learning.

The first principle is distinguishing reversible from irreversible decisions. Reversible decisions (what Amazon calls "two-way doors") should be made quickly by individuals or small groups close to the information. Irreversible decisions ("one-way doors") deserve slower deliberation, broader input, and more careful analysis. Most organizations make this distinction poorly: they deliberate endlessly on reversible decisions and rush through irreversible ones under time pressure.

The second principle is explicit decision rights. For any given decision, the team should be clear about who has the authority to decide, who must be consulted before the decision is made, who needs to be informed after, and who is responsible for executing. Ambiguity about decision rights is one of the most common sources of organizational friction — it produces either paralysis (no one decides) or conflict (multiple people decide differently).

The third principle is the decision log. After each significant decision, the team records what was decided, what the key considerations were, what alternatives were rejected and why, and what they expected to happen. Over time, the decision log becomes the team's most powerful learning tool — allowing them to see where their judgment was strong, where it was weak, and what patterns of error recur. Teams that build this discipline systematically improve their decision quality over months and years.
