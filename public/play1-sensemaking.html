Sensemaking Understanding where we are

Building a shared view of current reality before deciding where to go next.

The Foundation for Strategic Action

Sensemaking is how a team builds a shared view of current reality before deciding what to do next. Before a team can set direction, solve the right problems, or move with confidence, it needs an honest understanding of what is happening now.

This means slowing down long enough to separate facts from assumptions, signals from noise, and real uncertainty from stories we may be telling ourselves. Most teams are not starting from one shared picture. They are starting from partial views, different interpretations, and beliefs that may no longer hold.

That is why disagreement matters. When people see reality differently, it reveals where understanding is incomplete and where assumptions need to be tested. In this play, the goal is not immediate agreement. The goal is sharper collective understanding.

Core Principles

Separate facts, interpretations, assumptions, and unknowns
Look for the signals that matter most, not just the loudest inputs
Use differences in perspective to deepen understanding
Build a shared view of current reality before jumping to solutions
Resist premature alignment when key questions are still unresolved
Treat uncertainty as something to clarify, not something to hide
Resist the urge to converge prematurely on a unified narrative before the evidence supports one

Core Practices

Practice 1

FBSN Exercise (Facts, Beliefs, Signals, Noise)

Automated

The signature sensemaking tool. Teams surface their individual perspectives, then use a structured 2×2 matrix to categorize and prioritize what they know, believe, and need to act on.

Tutorial

▼

The FBSN Exercise is built around a 2×2 matrix with two critical axes. The horizontal axis measures Certainty — ranging from Fact (things we can verify and demonstrate) to Belief (things we hold to be true but cannot yet prove). The vertical axis measures Importance — from Signal (information that is genuinely consequential for our decisions) to Noise (information that is interesting but ultimately peripheral).

The process begins with individual input. Each team member contributes their perspective on a given strategic challenge or question — the raw observations, concerns, assumptions, and data points that shape how they see the situation. This divergent phase is critical: it captures the full range of perspectives before any filtering occurs.

Next, AI-assisted extraction identifies the distinct claims, observations, and assertions embedded in each contribution. This step transforms narrative input into discrete, categorizable items — making implicit assumptions visible and separating compound statements into their component parts.

The team then categorizes each item into the matrix. Facts that are also Signals become the bedrock of strategy — these are the things we know to be true and that genuinely matter. Beliefs that register as Signals are the most important items to test: they feel consequential, but we cannot yet be sure they are real. Facts that land as Noise are true but irrelevant. Beliefs that are Noise can be safely set aside.

Finally, the categorized matrix drives action planning. Items flow into three buckets: Must Do (actions demanded by confirmed, important facts), Might Do (experiments to test high-importance beliefs), and Won't Do (items the team consciously chooses to set aside). This discipline of explicit triage prevents the common failure of trying to act on everything simultaneously.

Practice 2

Three Horizons Scanning

Coming Soon

A framework for mapping the strategic landscape across three time horizons — what must be delivered today, what is emerging tomorrow, and what could transform the future.

Tutorial

▼

The Three Horizons framework provides a structured way for teams to see beyond the immediate and consider the full arc of strategic possibility. It was developed to help organizations hold multiple time perspectives simultaneously — something that leadership teams chronically struggle with, typically defaulting to the urgent at the expense of the important.

Horizon 1: Deliver Today. This is the current business — the products, services, processes, and capabilities that generate value right now. H1 work is about optimization, execution, and protecting what works. Every team spends most of its energy here, and for good reason. But H1 thinking alone leads to gradual decline as the world shifts around you.

Horizon 2: Build Tomorrow. These are the emerging opportunities that the team can see forming but that are not yet fully realized. H2 represents the bridge between the current business and the future — new markets, capabilities under development, partnerships being explored. H2 work requires investment before the returns are clear, which makes it politically vulnerable in most organizations.

Horizon 3: Create the Future. The transformational possibilities that could fundamentally reshape the organization or its industry. H3 thinking is speculative and generative — it explores what the world might look like in five, ten, or twenty years and what role the organization could play. Most teams dismiss H3 as impractical, but it is the source of breakthrough innovation.

In practice, teams use Three Horizons Scanning by mapping their current portfolio of initiatives, challenges, and strategic bets across all three horizons. The revealing question is not where the team is investing the most resources — but where it is investing the most leadership attention. Teams that starve H2 and H3 of senior focus are slowly writing their own obsolescence.

Practice 3

Assumption Mapping

Coming Soon

A rigorous practice for surfacing the implicit assumptions that silently drive strategic decisions — then deciding which ones most urgently need to be tested.

Tutorial

▼

Every strategy rests on a foundation of assumptions, and most of those assumptions are invisible to the people who hold them. Assumption Mapping is the practice of making the implicit explicit — dragging hidden beliefs into the light where they can be examined, challenged, and tested.

The process begins with elicitation. Each team member identifies the assumptions underlying a specific strategic decision, initiative, or belief about the future. The facilitator pushes for specificity: not "we assume the market will grow" but "we assume the European mid-market segment will grow at 8-12% annually through 2027." Vague assumptions cannot be tested; precise ones can.

Once assumptions are surfaced, the team rates each on two dimensions: confidence (how certain are we that this is true?) and consequence (how much does our strategy depend on this being true?). Assumptions that score low on confidence but high on consequence are the most dangerous — they are the load-bearing beams of strategy that might be rotten.

The final step is designing tests. For each high-risk assumption, the team identifies what evidence would confirm or refute it, where that evidence might be found, and how quickly it can be obtained. Some assumptions can be tested in days through customer conversations or data analysis. Others require structured experiments over weeks or months. The discipline is in matching the testing approach to the assumption's risk profile rather than defaulting to analysis paralysis or reckless optimism.
