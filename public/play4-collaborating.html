Collaborating Establishing the team's identity

Developing a distinctive way of working that becomes “how we work here.”

How the Team Works Together

The first three plays describe what value-creating teams do. Collaborating describes how they do it together. This is where the team develops its distinctive way of working — the patterns, rhythms, and practices that become "how we work here."

Like a musical ensemble that practices structured compositions to enable improvisation, teams build their collaborative capacity through deliberate practice. But there is a critical distinction: teams should not write down norms that are not already lived. Real norms emerge organically from experience.

Helps teams see themselves clearly — hidden assumptions, personal histories, values that anchor perspectives
Continuous reflection woven into the team's rhythm, not quarterly off-sites
The team's operating system: refining how they run plays, adapting to new challenges, building a distinctive identity
Collaborating is where the team becomes more than the sum of its members

Trust & Candor Protocol

Coming Soon

Building psychological safety and productive candor — the foundation for teams that can challenge each other's thinking without damaging relationships.

📚 Tutorial

▼

Psychological safety has become a popular concept, but most teams struggle to build it in practice. The Trust and Candor Protocol provides a structured, incremental approach. It begins with a simple premise: trust is built through small acts of vulnerability, not grand gestures of openness.

The protocol starts with low-stakes vulnerability exercises. Team members practice sharing what they don't know, what they're uncertain about, and where they need help — in domains where the risk feels manageable. This might begin with operational topics before moving to strategic or interpersonal ones. The key is that the team experiences what it feels like to be honest without consequence.

As trust builds, the protocol introduces productive candor practices. Teams learn to name what's not working without making it personal, to challenge each other's thinking without triggering defensiveness, and to admit uncertainty without losing credibility. Each of these is a specific skill that can be practiced and refined.

The protocol also addresses the most common failure mode: the gap between espoused values and actual behavior. Many teams say they value candor but punish it in practice. The protocol helps teams notice this gap — when someone raises a difficult truth, does the team lean in or pull back? When someone admits a mistake, is it held against them? These moments are where trust is either built or destroyed.

Over time, teams that practice this protocol develop a distinctive capacity: they can hold difficult conversations in real time, during the work, rather than needing separate "feedback sessions" or off-sites to say what's really on their minds. This is when candor becomes a competitive advantage — the team can course-correct faster because truth travels faster.

Collaborative Reflection

Coming Soon

Deeper inquiry into the team's patterns, blind spots, and emerging capabilities — not a standard retrospective, but a practice of learning to notice.

📚 Tutorial

▼

After significant decisions or events, the team pauses to reflect: How did we make that choice? What did we miss? What would we do differently? This practice draws on the tradition of the U.S. Army's After Action Review, but goes deeper — it's not just about what happened, but about the patterns of thinking and behavior that shaped what happened.

The practice begins immediately after a significant team moment — a major decision, a difficult meeting, a strategic pivot, or an unexpected outcome. The facilitator (which can rotate among team members) guides the reflection through three layers. First, the factual layer: what actually happened, in sequence? Teams often discover they have surprisingly different accounts of the same event.

Second, the pattern layer: what patterns of team behavior shaped this outcome? Did the team rush to consensus? Did one voice dominate? Did important perspectives go unheard? Did the team avoid a difficult topic? This layer requires the trust built through the Candor Protocol — team members need to be honest about dynamics they may have contributed to.

Third, the learning layer: what does this tell us about ourselves as a team? What capabilities are we building? What blind spots keep recurring? What would we do differently next time — not in terms of the decision itself, but in terms of how we worked together to make it?

Over time, teams that practice Collaborative Reflection develop a remarkable ability: they begin to notice their own dynamics in real time, not just in retrospect. Someone will say, "I think we're rushing to consensus again" or "We haven't heard from the people closest to this problem." This real-time self-awareness is the hallmark of a team that has truly internalized the Collaborating play — the team's operating system becomes self-correcting.
